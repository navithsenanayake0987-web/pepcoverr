Regulatory History

How we got here

The peptide regulatory situation has changed significantly in a short time.

Pre-2023
Peptides available through compounding pharmacies
Licensed compounding pharmacies could legally prepare many peptides with a physician's prescription. BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, and others were routinely prescribed by longevity and sports medicine clinics.
Late 2023
FDA moves 19 peptides to Category 2 — compounding banned
The FDA classified 19 widely-used peptides as Category 2 bulk drug substances, citing safety concerns and insufficient evidence. Compounding pharmacies were required to stop preparing them. Critics argued the FDA overstepped, noting no specific safety signal justified the broad restriction.
September 2024
5 peptides referred to PCAC for review
CJC-1295, Ipamorelin, Thymosin Alpha-1, AOD-9604, and Selank were referred to the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee — the first step toward potential Category 1 reclassification. This was a significant positive signal but not a change in legal status.
March 2025
Compounding of tirzepatide banned
Compounding pharmacies lost the ability to compound tirzepatide as the drug shortage designation was lifted. This pushed more demand underground and reinforced concerns about the FDA's approach to peptide access.
February 27, 2026 — Current situation
RFK announces reclassification of approximately 14 peptides
HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. announced on the Joe Rogan Experience that approximately 14 of the 19 restricted peptides would return to Category 1 status within "a couple of weeks." The announcement generated immediate consumer interest — but the formal FDA update has not been published. Legal status remains technically unchanged.
Expected — weeks to months from February 2026
Formal FDA reclassification published
When the FDA formally publishes the updated Category 1 list, licensed compounding pharmacies will be able to legally prepare the reclassified peptides with a physician's prescription. This page will be updated immediately.

How FDA categories work

Understanding the categories

These categories apply to compounding pharmacies — not to FDA drug approval, which is a completely separate process.

Category 1

Category 1 — Legal to compound

Substances on the Category 1 list can be legally prepared by licensed 503A compounding pharmacies with a valid patient-specific prescription from a licensed physician.

  • Requires physician prescription
  • Must use licensed compounding pharmacy
  • Does NOT mean FDA approved drug
  • Quality depends on the pharmacy
Category 2

Category 2 — Cannot be compounded

Substances the FDA has determined present potential safety risks or have insufficient evidence to permit compounding. Pharmacies cannot legally prepare them.

  • Cannot be legally compounded
  • Often sold as "research chemicals" — gray market
  • No pharmacy-grade quality control
  • Subject to change via reclassification
FDA Approved

FDA Approved — Full drug approval

Completely separate from compounding categories. FDA-approved peptide drugs have completed Phase I-III clinical trials and received formal New Drug Application approval.

  • Examples: Semaglutide, Tirzepatide, Insulin
  • Proven safety and efficacy in large trials
  • Available via standard prescription
  • 80+ peptide drugs are FDA approved
WADA Banned

WADA Banned — Sports prohibition

The World Anti-Doping Agency separately prohibits many peptides for competitive athletes. WADA bans are independent of FDA categories.

  • Applies to competitive athletes only
  • Does not affect civilian legal status
  • TB-500, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin are banned
  • Testing is increasingly sophisticated
