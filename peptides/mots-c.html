Overview

What is MOTS-c?

MOTS-c (Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame of the 12S rRNA type-c) is a mitochondria-derived peptide encoded in the mitochondrial genome. It was only discovered in 2015 — making it one of the newest peptides on this site. Unlike most peptides which are derived from larger proteins, MOTS-c is encoded directly in mitochondrial DNA.

Its discovery was significant because it revealed that mitochondria produce their own signaling peptides that communicate with the rest of the cell and body. MOTS-c appears to regulate energy metabolism, insulin sensitivity, and exercise capacity — and circulating levels decline significantly with age.

It is sometimes called the 'exercise peptide' because its plasma levels rise dramatically with physical exercise. This has led to both research interest in its role in exercise adaptation and concern from anti-doping authorities who are monitoring it.

Scientific Evidence

What the research actually says

MOTS-c promotes metabolic homeostasis and reduces obesity
Moderate (animal)
Cell Metabolism · 2015 · Lee et al.
The discovery paper. MOTS-c injection in mice prevented age-related and diet-induced obesity and improved insulin sensitivity. Strong mechanistic data in animals established the foundation for further research.
View on PubMed →
MOTS-c levels correlate with exercise and aging in humans
Moderate
Nature Aging · 2020 · Multiple authors
Human observational study showing MOTS-c levels rise with exercise and decline with age. Higher circulating MOTS-c correlates with better metabolic health. Not an intervention study — shows association not causation.
View on PubMed →
MOTS-c for insulin resistance — mouse models
Moderate (animal)
Diabetes · 2016–2020 · Multiple studies
Multiple mouse studies show MOTS-c improves insulin sensitivity, activates AMPK pathway, and reduces liver fat accumulation. Mechanism is well characterized in animals.

Summary

The bottom line

MOTS-c is the most cutting-edge peptide on this site — discovered in 2015, it is still in very early research stages. The animal evidence for metabolic benefits and the human observational data are genuinely exciting, but this is a peptide where the science is truly in its infancy. People using it now are essentially early adopters of a compound with almost no human intervention data. The longevity community's interest is understandable given its mitochondrial mechanism, but caution is warranted. More than any other peptide here, MOTS-c represents a significant unknown.
