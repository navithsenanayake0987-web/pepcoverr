Overview

What is AOD-9604?

AOD-9604 stands for Anti-Obesity Drug 9604. It is a modified fragment of human growth hormone (hGH) — specifically the C-terminal fragment corresponding to amino acids 176-191. This region of hGH is responsible for its fat-burning (lipolytic) properties, while the fragment does not carry the growth-promoting or diabetogenic effects of full hGH.

It was originally developed by Monash University in Australia specifically as a weight loss drug. It reached Phase IIb and Phase III clinical trials — which is unusually advanced for peptides in this category. However, it failed to demonstrate sufficient efficacy to gain FDA approval for obesity treatment.

Despite failing regulatory approval, AOD-9604 remains popular in the biohacking community as a fat loss aid, under the reasoning that even if its effect wasn't large enough for pharmaceutical approval, some fat-burning benefit may still be useful for individuals.

Scientific Evidence

What the research actually says

AOD-9604 Phase IIb trial for obesity — 536 patients
Moderate
Obesity Research · 2004 · Ng et al.
The largest clinical trial of AOD-9604. 536 obese patients randomized to multiple doses vs placebo. Showed modest but statistically significant fat loss at certain doses over 12 weeks. Failed to show clinically meaningful weight loss in later Phase III trials.
View on PubMed →
AOD-9604 and adipose tissue lipolysis — mechanism studies
Moderate
Endocrinology · Multiple studies
Mechanism studies confirm AOD-9604 stimulates lipolysis (fat breakdown) via beta-3 adrenergic receptors — the same pathway targeted by some weight loss drugs. Does not affect blood sugar or IGF-1 levels.
View on PubMed →
Phase III trial — did not meet primary endpoint
Negative result
Regulatory submission · 2005
Phase III trial failed to demonstrate clinically sufficient weight loss for FDA approval. Development as a pharmaceutical was halted. The compound works mechanistically but effect size was insufficient for drug approval standards.

Summary

The bottom line

AOD-9604 is unusual in that it actually went through large-scale clinical trials — reaching Phase III with 536 patients in Phase IIb. The mechanism is real and confirmed. The problem is effect size: it works, but not enough to meet the bar for pharmaceutical obesity treatment. Whether a sub-pharmaceutical effect is still useful for individuals is a matter of personal judgment. Its safety profile is one of the cleaner ones in this space — no IGF-1 elevation, no glucose effects. Community scores are lower than most peptides on this site, reflecting the modest real-world effects users experience.
