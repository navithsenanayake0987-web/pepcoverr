About PepCover

Built because the information online is broken

Peptide interest has exploded. The information has not kept up. Reddit is unreliable, most sites are selling something, and the research is scattered and hard to understand. PepCover exists to fix that.

Mission

What we are trying to do

One clear goal — be the most trustworthy source of peptide information on the internet.

Peptides are at an interesting moment. Search interest has crossed 10 million monthly searches in the US. A major regulatory shift just happened. Influencers are promoting them with affiliate codes. Doctors are quietly prescribing them. Biohackers have been self-experimenting for years.

And yet if you try to research a specific peptide, you end up on Reddit sorting through contradictory anecdotes, or on a supplement seller's site reading marketing dressed up as science, or on PubMed trying to parse academic papers written for researchers not patients.

PepCover does something different. We combine two layers of truth for every peptide — what the peer-reviewed science actually says, and what real people actually experience. We score both independently. We are honest when evidence is weak. We do not sell supplements. We do not take affiliate money from peptide companies.

Methodology

How we calculate scores

Two independent scores. Neither replaces the other.

🔬

Science Score — 1 to 10

The Science Score rates the quality of published human research for a given peptide and application. It is based entirely on human clinical data — not animal studies, not in vitro research, not mechanistic plausibility. A peptide with 100 promising animal studies and zero human trials will score low.

This is intentional. Animal evidence often does not translate to humans. Our score reflects what we actually know about effects in people.

  • 1
    Number of human trials — how many published studies involved human subjects
  • 2
    Sample sizes — a trial with 500 participants weighs more than a trial with 12
  • 3
    Study design quality — placebo-controlled randomized trials score higher than retrospective case series
  • 4
    Consistency of results — multiple independent studies showing the same effect increases confidence
  • 5
    Regulatory history — pharmaceutical approval in any jurisdiction is a strong positive signal
Example — Thymosin Alpha-1 scores 8/10
Approved in 35+ countries+3
Multiple placebo-controlled RCTs+3
Decades of safety data+2
No large Western RCTs-2
Final score8/10
👥

Community Score — % positive

The Community Score aggregates real user experiences from Reddit, forum discussions, and community reports. Our AI processes thousands of posts, extracting structured data — peptide used, goal, outcome, dosage, side effects — and aggregates them into percentage scores by use case.

We show the community score separately from the science score because they measure different things. High community consensus despite weak science might signal that the animal evidence is translating to humans but hasn't been formally studied yet. Low community consensus despite strong science might signal that individual response varies widely.

  • 1
    Outcome extraction — AI classifies each report as positive, negative, neutral, or mixed
  • 2
    Use case filtering — scores are broken down by goal (tendon recovery, gut health, etc.) not combined
  • 3
    Confidence weighting — low-confidence extractions are excluded from scores
  • 4
    Minimum threshold — we require at least 50 data points before showing a score publicly
  • 5
    No usernames stored — we aggregate patterns only, never attribute data to individuals

Editorial principles

How we make decisions

Rules we follow on every page we publish.

01

We never oversell benefits

If a peptide has only animal studies, we say so clearly. We do not write "shown to heal tendons" when the evidence comes from rat models. We write "shown to accelerate tendon healing in rat models." The distinction matters.

02

We cite primary sources

Every study referenced on this site links to the original paper on PubMed — not to another website's summary of the paper. We encourage you to read the source yourself.

03

We update when things change

The peptide regulatory landscape is changing fast. Every page shows a "last updated" date. The regulation tracker is updated whenever there is an official regulatory change.

04

We separate anecdote from evidence

Community scores are clearly labeled as anecdotal. They are never presented as clinical evidence. The science score and community score are always displayed separately — never merged into a single number.

05

We are not medical advisors

Nothing on this site is medical advice. We provide research information. Decisions about whether to use a peptide, at what dose, and for what purpose should be made with a licensed physician who knows your medical history.

What we get wrong

Our known limitations

Transparency includes being honest about where we fall short.

Community data has inherent bias. People who had positive experiences are more likely to post. People who had no effect often just stop using and never report. Our community scores may overrepresent positive outcomes.
AI extraction is imperfect. Our pipeline processes Reddit posts through AI to extract structured data. The AI occasionally misclassifies outcomes or misses context. We spot-check regularly but cannot guarantee 100% accuracy.
Research moves fast. New peptide studies are published regularly. There may be a lag between a study being published and our pages being updated. Always check PubMed for the most recent research.
Science scores are simplified. Reducing evidence quality to a single number inevitably loses nuance. A peptide with one well-designed RCT in 200 people may score similarly to one with five small poorly-designed studies. We try to explain the nuance in the text.
Regulation changes faster than we can track. We update manually when we are aware of changes. Subscribe to our newsletter to be notified of regulatory updates as soon as we publish them.
